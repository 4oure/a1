Chicago's Justice Gap

Crime in Chicago fell nearly 50% since 2001 — but the arrest rate fell even faster. Brush Chart 1 to filter Charts 2 and 3 by year range.

Chart 1 — Crime Volume & Arrest Rate (2001–2025)

Bars = total crimes. Line = arrest rate %. Brush to filter the charts below.

The gray bars show how total reported crime has declined steadily over the past two decades and how it has been nearly cut in half since 2001. But the red line tells a more troubling story, which is that the percentage of crimes that actually result in an arrest has fallen even faster. Two moments stand out. In 2016, following the release of the Laquan McDonald shooting video, arrest rates dropped sharply as public scrutiny of the Chicago Police Department intensified. Then in 2020, the combined effect of COVID-19 and the George Floyd/BLM protests pushed the arrest rate to an all-time low. A lower crime rate doesn't always mean more accountability, it just means fewer crimes and fewer arrests. Use the brush to select a specific time period and see how the breakdown changes in the charts below.

Chart 2 — Crime Type Breakdown (All Years)

Total crimes vs. arrests per crime type. Updates when you brush Chart 1.

This chart breaks down the justice gap by crime type. The gray bar shows how many incidents were reported; the blue bar shows how many led to an arrest. For crimes like Narcotics, the bars are nearly equal because police largely only record a narcotics incident when they make an arrest. But for the highest-volume crimes such as Theft, Battery, and Criminal Damage, the blue bar barely registers against the gray. Because these crimes are more likely to be reported by other civilians and not by cops upon arrest. The crimes most likely to affect everyday Chicagoans are also the ones least likely to be solved. Try brushing different time periods in Chart 1 to see whether this gap has grown or shrunk over the years.

Chart 3 — Arrest Rate by Crime Type (All Years)

Which crimes lead to arrests? The most common crimes are the least likely to be solved.

When sorting by arrest rate, the issue is made clear. Narcotics, Gambling, and Prostitution sit near 100%, but this is largely because these crimes are only recorded when police are already making an arrest. Meanwhile, Burglary sits at under 6%, Motor Vehicle Theft under 8%, and Theft, the single most common crime in Chicago with over 1.8 million incidents, leads to an arrest less than 11% of the time. Meaning if something is stolen from you and you report it, there is barely over a 10% chance that the thief will be caught and an even lower percentage that he or she will be convicted. The dashed line marks the citywide average. Most of the crimes that define daily life in Chicago fall well below that average.

About the Dataset

This visualization uses the Chicago Crimes dataset (2001–present) from the Chicago Data Portal, extracted from the Chicago Police Department's CLEAR system. The raw dataset contains over 8.5 million rows. We pre-aggregated it to yearly counts by crime type for browser performance. We focused on three variables: total number of crimes, number of arrests, and arrest rate, all broken down by crime type and year.

Design Rationale

A dual-axis bar and line chart for chart 1 was chosen because we needed to show two different quantities, both total crime volume and arrest rate percentage, on the same timeline without one obscuring the other. The coloring on the bars is intentionally a muted color so that the red arrest rate line remains the primary focus of a reader's attention.

Chart 2 uses overlapping horizontal bars rather than grouped vertical bars because the crime type labels can be pretty long and read more naturally on the y-axis. Layering arrests on top of total crimes in a contrasting color makes the gap between the two immediately visible without requiring the user to do mental math. Seeing the sharp disparity between two colors is likely to bring about a notice in the disparity.

Chart 3 uses a color scale mapped to arrest rate percentage so that low-accountability crimes read as visually alarming. It is sorted by arrest rate so the most and least accountable crimes are on opposite ends of the graph. An average line is provided for the users as a reference point.

Implemented Interactions

Category I — Brushing & Linking: Chart 1 includes a D3 brushX interaction. When a user selects a year range, both chart 2 and 3 automatically recompute and draw only using data from the selected years.
Category II — Tooltips: all 3 charts include mouseover tooltips where hovering over a bar or other part of the graph reveals exact details for that data point. This is done to improve readability and clarity without filling the chart with a bunch of clutter and labels.
Category II — Annotations: Chart 1 and 3 include permanent annotations to draw attention to specific information that can help contextualize each graph.
